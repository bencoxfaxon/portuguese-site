Presente do Indicativo: the present tense indicates an action that takes place in the moment of narration, or may indicate habitual action.

Other forms of speaking in the present:

Presente Progressivo: the present progressive tense is used to describe an event that is occuring during the moment of discourse.

Formed by: ESTAR (in the Presente) + Present Participle

English: am/are + Present Participle (going, seeing, doing, eating)

Example: I am eating in the cafeteria now.

Other forms of speaking in the present:

Forming Present Participles: Present participles are words in English ending in '-ing.' They are easy to make in Portuguese. Drop the 'ar,' 'er,' or 'ir' from the infinitive then add:

AR ER IR
+ 'ando' + 'endo' + 'indo'
chegando (arriving) comendo (eating) partindo (leaving)

ESTAR in the Presente

Eu estou
Você está
Nós estamos
Vocês estão

Pretérito Mais-Que-Perfeito Composto: The Portuguese pluperfect is used for describing actions in the past that precedes another action in the past. Typically, both actions are represented in the same sentence: the preceeding action is in the pluperfect (had + past participle), while the antecedent action is in the past perfect.

Formed by: TER (in the Imperfeito) + Past Participle

English: had + Past Participle (gone, seen, done, eaten)

Example: I had already left when you arrived.

Uses:

Forming Past Participles: Present participles are typically words in English ending in '-ed.' However, there are several excpetions for English. For most Portuguese verbs, drop the 'ar,' 'er,' or 'ir' from the infinitive then add:

AR ER IR
+ 'ado' + 'ido' + 'ido'
chegado (arrived) comido (eaten) partido (left)

TER in the Imperfeito

Você tinha
Nós tínhamos
Vocês tinham

Voz Passiva: the passive voice is used to describe an action in the past that happens to an object.

Formed by: SER (in the Perfeito or the Imperfeito) + Past Participle (adjusted for gender and plurality)

English: was/were + Past Participle (made, sent, given, ordered)

Example: The boats were made of wood.

Uses:

Forming Past Participles: Present participles are typically words in English ending in '-ed.' However, there are several excpetions for English. For most Portuguese verbs, drop the 'ar,' 'er,' or 'ir' from the infinitive then add:

AR ER IR
+ 'ado' + 'ido' + 'ido'
chegado (arrived) comido (eaten) partido (left)

Gender and Plurality: Participles used in the Voz Passiva will be adjusted based on gender and plurality. If describing a feminine object, the ending will be 'a'; for masculine objects it will remain 'o.' When describing more than one object the ending will also contain an 's.' For example, here is are the past participles of FAZER adjusted for gender and plurality: feito (m, s); feita (f, s); feitos (m, p); feitas (f, p).

Presente do Subjuntivo: the Portuguese presente subjunctive is used when expressing HOPE, ADVICE, WANT, NEED, and DOUBT. Additionally, use this verb form when GIVING COMMANDS. The Subjuntivo in Portuguese represents a continuum of liklihoods for the actions they are describing. There is (1) will be happening, represented by the Futuro do Subjuntivo; (2) plausible or likely to happen, represented by the Presente do Subjuntivo; and (3) extremely unlikely or hypothetical, represented by the Imperfeito do Subjuntivo. Because the Presente do Subjuntivo describes plausible situations with present verbs it is ideal for describing situations involving hope, want, and need. Note: all verbs in the Presente do Subjuntivo are in the present, to describe actions happening just before the present, use Sub. Pres. of 'ter' + past participle. Helpful Link: Semantica Portuguese

Other forms of speaking in the present:

Signal Phrases: the part of the sentence expressing hope, want, and need is in the Presente, while the second part of the sentence is the verb in the Presente do Subjuntivo. Objeto Indireito: the indirect object is an object that has an action done to, by, or for it.

Preposition and Object Relation: PARA (for, to); A (to); DE (from); POR (by)

Pronoun Objeto Indireito If used with a preposition:
Eu me para mim
Você lhe, te para você, ele, ela
Nós nos para nós
Vocês lhes para vocês, elas, eles

Placement: There are two things to note with the placement of the indirect object: 1: The indirect object comes right after the verb if the sentence is used without 'para' and the sentence only has one verb. The exception to this is 'me,' 'te,' and 'nos,' which are used before the verb. 2: However, if there exists two verbs the indirect object comes between them.

Objeto Direito: the direct object is a person or thing that recieves the action. In English, the object is usally right after the verb.

'S,' 'Z,' 'R' Transformations: The object in Portuguese usually comes right before the verb, unless the verb ends with an 's,' 'r,' or 'z' sound, in which case the object is attached to the end of the verb. This usually occurs when the infinitive is employed.

Pronoun Objeto Objeto if Verb ends in 'S' 'R' 'Z' Objeto - Formal
Eu me
Você o, a, te (te = informal) -lo, -la o senhor, a senhora, donna
Nós nos
Vocês os, as -los, -las as senhoras, os senhores

Making the transformation for the verb requires dropping the 'ar/ir/er' from the infinitive, then adding: í- (ir) á- (ar) and ê- (er).
